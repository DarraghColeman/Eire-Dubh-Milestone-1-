The Diaspora


A small island with a big footprint.

From Tipperary to Timbuktu, Mayo to Machu Pichu, the Irish are all over. It is estimated that up to 80 million people worldwide claim Irish heritage, making it one of the largest diasporas in the world. Even more astonishingly is the fact that those this diaspora dwarves Ireland itself being 13 times larger than the population on the island itself.


The vast majority of Irish emigrants today are young, well-educated and skilled people coming from an affluent country. They leave seeking opportunity, adventure or education abroad. With internet and travel more accessible and cheap by the day, it's relatively easy for emigrants to keep in touch with loved ones and to take trips home.


However, this was not always the case as our ancestors did not have such privilages coming from extremely impoverished circumstances. Many went from poverty in Ireland to poverty in a new land. Even worse still, many were exiled for their religion or political beliefs and forced into a life of servitude or slavery. Less than a century ago, an Irish person leaving their native country would be assumed to never set foot on it again.

Today the Irish are broadly welcomed around the world, but often Irish communities abroad were treated with disdain and discrimination. Many found themselves in segregated slums which lead to the formation of organised crime gangs.

Others, seeing their misfortunes, continued travelling and sought opportunity elsewhere. Therefore, it's important to consider the circumstances which led to the making of a some of our featured forgotten friends.



With the most remote Irish pubs not being in Donegal, the Kerry mountains or Aran Islands, but in the Himalayas, Patagonia, Mongolia and the Andes... it's clear to see how far Irish feet have travelled.

However, there are a number of countries in which the Irish mainly settled, either by choice, necessity or by force. Check out the table below to see events that caused the initial migrations and to where they went.

Today



Over the last decade there has been a steady flow of Irish emigrants all across the globe. However in recent times, the reasons for leaving have changed from economic necessity to include better and more varied opportunities abroad, the cost of living and housing crisis in Ireland, chance to acrue wealth, adventure and a retirement in the sun. In addition to the above countries, today many Irish people are deciding to settle in the Middle-East, South-East Asia and the Mediterranean.
